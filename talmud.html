Talmud
The idea of the hyperlink, if not limited by its role in a digital format, goes far further back than Ted Nelson or Vannevar Bush. The Talmud, with its heavy use of annotations and nested commentary over rabbinic generations, and the Ramayana and Mahabharata, with their branching discourse, are early examples of how hypertext directs thought (Soumen).

Both Talmudic scholarship and hypertext imply a way of knowing that is different from the linear format that writing often takes. It acknowledges through its many margin annotations that there is not one answer to the open propositions of the ancient original text and that thought is layered, shaped by generational changes.

A typical Talmud page combines original text, oral tradition, commentary, annotation, and references to other religious texts in ways that are "busy, nonlinear, filled with different typefaces, graphical symbols, parallel and intersecting frames, and even multiple languages" (Weiss). The natural left to right pathway that the eye takes when reading is disrupted in the Talmud, with the reader being interrupted by sections of commentary. Often, you find yourself jumping back and forth in the text for an answer that came earlier than the question itself (Espen Aarseth, 1994). It attempts to contain all relevant comments on the original text to a single page, therefore gathering information over time into nested and interlinked segments.

Not only does the Talmud embrace physical nonlinearity, but it allows a text thousands of years old to be reframed over time through annotation meaning the text in itself cannot be bound by time. If we look at this from a hypertext point of view, this is a fantastic analog for a modern [wiki]. The Talmud also acknowledges that the text belongs to no individual as the identity of commentators is lost over time to the summary of all their thoughts. They are cross-referenced, allowing religious knowledge to become an open paradigm. The internet, too, is an ever-changing public interface of edits.

The hyperlink is a continual process of reinvention with clear precursors (Turow). It is a format of thought rather than something to separate the digital from the printed. These ancient handwritten texts and the HTML coded versions we see today are not so different after all, and when we look past the physical vs digital dichotomy, we can see an underlying desire to break the bounds of time and constraints of linear thought.
